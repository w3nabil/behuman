# Evidences

---

This is evidence page for PSPM calculation and `become-human`'s requested file that runs each time machine triggers, not just one time if you have multiple paragraphs. This file can not be triggered or used if you do not have any content and/or text for your work.

## Strong Evidence

* Is there at least one naturally awkward or inelegant sentence?
* Are very short and very long sentences mixed throughout the text?
* Does the author occasionally jump between ideas without a perfect transition?
* Does the paper switch between simple and advanced vocabulary?
* Does the writing style noticeably fluctuate between sections?
* Are there small structural inconsistencies between similar sentences?
* Does the text contain subjective observations, hesitations, or personal reasoning?
* Does the text contain bias of decision or reasoning which is not in the middle point?

## Partial Evidence

* Are examples specific and concrete rather than generic?
* Do paragraph lengths vary noticeably throughout the text?
* Does the conclusion introduce a new perspective instead of only summarizing?
* Are some arguments developed more deeply than others?
* Does informal language occasionally appear?
* Does repetition occur naturally rather than in a formulaic pattern?
* Does the text contain unusual or idiosyncratic expressions?
* Are there minor grammatical, punctuation, or formatting inconsistencies?
* Is there evidence of a unique personal voice or perspective?

> I partially agree with the statement that problem-solving and creative thinking are more important than coding skills. These skills are useful in every field, including education, business, healthcare, science, and social work. Without programmers, many modern innovations would not exist. Coding also encourages logical thinking, attention to detail, and systematic problem-solving, which are valuable skills in themselves.
>
> At the same time, coding without problem-solving skills has limitations. A person may know several programming languages but still struggle to create meaningful solutions if they cannot understand problems or think creatively. Similarly, someone with excellent ideas may find it difficult to implement them without technical knowledge. This shows that coding and problem-solving should not be viewed as opposing skills; instead, they complement each other.
>
> In my opinion, the ideal situation is to develop both abilities. Problem-solving and creativity help people discover what needs to be done, while coding provides a way to build and implement those solutions. A person who possesses all three qualities—creative thinking, problem-solving ability, and coding skills—is often better equipped to contribute to society and address real-world challenges. Therefore, rather than debating which skill is more important, it is better to recognize that both play essential roles in creating a better world.
